MOOC

I am proud of being an active learner. I was fortunate to enroll in Prof. Ping-Cheng (Benson) Yeh's flipped-classroom undergraduate Probabilistic course in my sophomore year. Prof. Yeh founded the Lab of Teaching Innovation (NTUTI, now part of the Digital Learning Center) at NTU. In my senior year (2015), I joined NTUTI as a MOOC Explorer (ME), a part-time position for active-learning students to 1) help in manufacturing the NTU MOOCs on Coursera and 2) popularize active/online/innovated learning within and beyond the campus.

We had a study group on MOOC-related research on principles of online learning, and also MOOC-specific topics such as enhancing engagements, operating course communities, open-source vs. charging mechanisms (one-time payment, subscription, etc). At NTUTI, I found my master thesis topic: enhancing grading efforts in peer assessments. Here are a series of column pieces I wrote on this topic (in Mandarin), and also my my master thesis where I tried to formulate a game theoretical model as a foundation of this kind of mechanism.

The most significant accomplishment during my time at NTUTI was probably the Mini-MOOCs, a learning-by-doing project where every ME designed a compact yet complete MOOC module so as to experience every bit of trouble that MOOC designers face in course development. In December 2016, I co-organized and paneled the Why MOOCs? workshop at NTU. My project, Braess’ Paradox, and the inefficient world (in Mandarin) focused on two messages: 1) MOOC is not equivalent to video-teaching, and can succeed even without any videos; 2) Designing activities in MOOCs is fundamentally different from traditional activity design in offline courses and must emphasize on stimulating and incentivizing learner activeness. We then participated in the 2017 ZaShare exhibition at Taipei, where my Mini-MOOC was one of the three works exhibited.

Other things I did at NTUTI included helping the NTU eProfessor workshop on flipped classroom, peer-to-peer learning, and teaching innovation technology. Overall, this is an inspring experience that shapes a lot of myself as an active learner and researcher.

Coresets Meet EDCS, and Introduction to distributed/MPC/streaming models,
Theory Study Group, IIS, March-May, 2019 [Overview Slide(PPT)] [description]

    From March to May 2019, I organized a semi-study group on distributed computing, Massive Parallel Computing (MPC), and streaming models. The goal was to learn from frontier theory works on advanced (distributed/MPC/streaming) models. Togetherly we start from a SODA'19 paper, Coresets Meet EDCS: Algorithms for Matching and Vertex Cover on Massive Graphs , then slowly traverse its origin to some SODA'16 and SPAA'17 papers. Special thanks to all the authors (S. Assadi, A. Bernstein, S. Khanna, C. Stein) to make their valuable research results publicly available as comprehensive slides! The slide above is an overview of this line of research, and it contains material from their original slides.

An Introduction to Streaming Models and Probabilistic Methods,
Theory Study Group, IIS, March 8, 2018 [Slides(PDF)] [description]

    I gave a brief introduction to the data streaming computation model, including the basic majority and heavy-hitter problems. This talk also includes some useful probabilistic methods such as the union bound and the Lovasz Local Lema (LLL) and its usage in hypergraph coloring. The material came from Prof. Meng-Tsung Tsai's course and also other textbooks.

A survey on Randomized Algorithms on Influence Maximization,
Theory Study Group, IIS, Aug 2, 2017 [Slides(PDF)] [description]

    A brief survey on state-of-the-art approaches for the classical influence maximization optimization problem, including the IC and LT models, the classical NP-hard optimizing problem, the #P-hard computing problem on estimating the influence spread, Monte-Carlo sampling algorithms, Reverse Sampling approach, and some extensions and applications, e.g, on rumor blocking.

Anti-Coordination Games and its Inefficiency,
Collborative Algorithms Group, NTU, Mar 28, 2016 [Slides(PDF)] [description]

    Anti-coordination games capture the scenarios where players want to avoid making the same decision with others. Consider graph coloring as a distributed game, or the scenario where multiple groups of graduate students scheduling the experiment devices. This survey includes the inefficiency (PoA's and Strong PoA's) in such games, where Strong PoA extends the idea of PoA to ruling out multiple player collaboratively deviate together (recall that Nash Equilibria rules out only single deviation).

Between Simultaneity and Sequentiality,
Collborative Algorithms Group, NTU, Dec 18, 2015 [Slides(PDF)] [description]

    The Price-of-Anarchy (PoA) captures the cost of selfish behavior in (simultaneous) decentralized systems by comparing the socially optimal outcome with the Nash Equilibria. If the game is played sequentially instead of simultaneously, we can compare the socially optimal outcome to Subgame Perfect Nash Equilibria (SPNE) and get Sequential Price-of-Anarchy (SPoA). For sequential games, SPNE is a superset of NE. However, there exist games with SPoA > PoA and also games with SPoA < PoA. Why and how? This is a survey of several classes of games on this topic.

Braess's Paradox in Random Networks,
Collborative Algorithms Group, NTU, Oct 23, 2015 [Slides(PDF)] [description]

    Braess's paradox is a counterintuitive phenomenon in selfish routing games: the efficiency of the whole system (or, the total social welfare) decreases when a new resource is introduced and can be leveraged by all players. It has practical applications in transportation, electricity networks, mechanics, and also basketball(!). This survey focuses on how likely it happens in random wired networks, and how to detect it. I love this survey particularly since it connects algorithmic game theory with practical issues in transportation. I look forward to visiting those locations in Boston and Seoul (real-world examples of Braess's Paradox) oneday. After this survey, I did a Mini-MOOC on exactly this topic including running a real simple physical experiment.
